## Hero Section

**Eyebrow:** *Specialist Therapy*

**Concern:** This is generic and under-used. The eyebrow is prime real estate — it's the first text a visitor reads. "Specialist Therapy" could describe almost any therapist. This label should do more work to orient the reader immediately.

**Suggestions:**
- *Therapy for Professionals* — simple, direct, self-selects the right reader
- *Online Therapy, London & Worldwide* — leads with availability, which is a differentiator
- *Relational & Psychodynamic Therapy* — leads with methodology for readers who already know what they're looking for

**Headline:** *Therapist for High Achieving Professionals*

**Notes:** The word "High Achieving" is the right territory but has two small tensions:
1. Many of the people this site is designed to reach are *questioning* their achievements, experiencing burnout, or have started to feel that achievement hasn't delivered what they expected. Leading with "High Achieving" as a label they might be ambivalent about could create a small distance.
2. It's slightly corporate-adjacent — "high achieving" is language from performance reviews and LinkedIn.

**Alternatives worth considering:**
- *Therapist for Driven Professionals* — implies forward motion without the achievement pressure
- *Therapist for People Who Have Everything Except Enough Time to Feel Okay* — much longer but very specific, could be powerful as a subhead rather than headline
- *Therapist for Professionals* — simpler; the work of the paragraph below can do the qualifying

**However:** "High Achieving" is also clear, searchable, and your target reader will recognise themselves in it. If you feel this describes your clients well, it works. This is worth a conversation rather than a straight swap.

**Do you want to keep "High Achieving"? Any alternative language that resonates more?**

## What I Offer

**Item 1 lead sentence:** *I work with high-achieving professionals navigating the pressures of demanding careers.*

**Notes:** This is a full sentence that functions simultaneously as a heading and as the opening of a paragraph. The second sentence continues it: *"Particularly in finance, consultancy, and corporate environments."* This works conceptually but reads slightly fragmented — the lead sentence makes a broad claim, and the next sentence narrows it. Consider either: (a) merging them into one opening sentence, or (b) reframing the lead as a topic label ("Who I Work With") and starting the paragraph with the full sentence.

**What's your preference for how this is structured?**

**Item 2:** *Coaching-adjacent therapy.*

**Notes:** "Coaching-adjacent" is interesting and slightly unusual — which is part of its value. It signals to the reader who has considered coaching that this is on their radar. However, it may be unfamiliar to readers who haven't thought in those terms, and "adjacent" can read as evasive ("it's almost coaching but not quite").

The body copy here is one of the strongest passages on the page: *"Many people seek coaching thinking it's about work — burnout, performance pressure, or a challenging decision. But often it's about deeper patterns..."* This is genuinely insightful and will resonate with the right reader.

**Suggestion:** Consider whether "Coaching-adjacent" earns its place as a heading, or whether something that reflects the paragraph's actual content works better — e.g. *More Than Coaching* or *When Coaching Isn't Enough*.

**Happy with "Coaching-adjacent"? Or would you prefer to explore alternatives?**

**Item 3:** *My approach is relational and depth-oriented.*

**Notes:** Clear and accurate. "Depth-oriented" is good — it signals something that goes beyond surface-level techniques without using jargon. The body copy explains it well. No changes needed here.

**Item 4:** *I draw on psychodynamic, person-centred, CBT, Gestalt, and attachment-based approaches.*

**Notes:** This lead sentence is the most technical on the page, and it's the last in the list — so the reader has been warmed up by now. The body copy does good work of translating: *"we pay attention not only to what you bring, but also to what emerges between us in the room."* This is effective. However, leading with a list of modalities ("psychodynamic, person-centred, CBT...") reads as a credentials statement rather than a client-facing benefit. The body copy is the more compelling statement of what this means in practice.

**Suggestion:** Consider leading with the more human sentence and letting the modalities follow, or naming the modalities in the body copy rather than the lead.

## Broader Observations

**Language consistency:** The site alternates between "therapy" and "therapist" (correct) and "sessions" — this is consistent throughout. Good.

**Accessibility of language:** The copy is written at approximately a degree-educated reading level, which is appropriate for the target audience. However, someone arriving during a moment of distress may find the density of methodological language (psychodynamic, relational, integrative) slightly distancing. There is nowhere on the page a vulnerable or uncertain reader is simply told: *"it's okay to reach out."* Even one sentence in the contact section — something like "You don't need to have it figured out before you get in touch" — could make a meaningful difference for someone who almost didn't make contact.

**A note on gender:** The site uses "people" and "you" throughout — no gendered assumptions. Good. The hero image (you) and bio are of a woman, and the copy never assumes the reader's gender. This is correct.

**Missing:** There is no mention of what the first session looks like, or what to expect. For many people, the uncertainty of what actually happens in a therapy session is a barrier. A single sentence — even in the contact section — that says something like "We'll start with an initial conversation, no preparation needed" could reduce that friction significantly.
